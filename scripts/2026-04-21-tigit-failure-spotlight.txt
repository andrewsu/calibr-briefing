Today we're going deep on what might be the definitive end of one of immuno-oncology's biggest bets — the TIGIT hypothesis. Gilead has announced it's loosening its partnership ties with Arcus Biosciences after their TIGIT inhibitor program failed in Phase 3. And this isn't just one company's setback — it may mark the closing of a chapter that consumed billions of dollars and years of industry effort across multiple pharma giants.

So let's unpack what happened, why it matters, and what it tells us about where cancer immunotherapy goes from here.

First, the basics. TIGIT, which stands for T-cell immunoreceptor with immunoglobulin and ITIM domain, is an immune checkpoint receptor — similar conceptually to PD-1 and CTLA-4, the targets behind blockbuster drugs like Keytruda and Opdivo. The theory was straightforward and compelling: if blocking PD-1 unleashes T cells against cancer, then blocking TIGIT alongside PD-1 could unleash them even further. It was supposed to be the next big combination play in immuno-oncology.

The excitement peaked around 2020 to 2022, when Roche's tiragolumab showed encouraging early data in non-small cell lung cancer. That triggered a gold rush. Gilead partnered with Arcus in a deal worth up to five billion dollars. Bristol Myers Squibb, Merck, and multiple other companies poured resources into their own TIGIT programs. At its peak, there were probably fifteen or more TIGIT antibodies in clinical development across the industry.

But the Phase 3 data has been relentlessly disappointing. Roche's tiragolumab failed multiple pivotal trials. BMS pulled back on their TIGIT program. And now Gilead's decision to loosen ties with Arcus after this latest Phase 3 miss adds another data point to what's become an overwhelming pattern of failure.

The core problem appears to be that TIGIT's biology is more complex than the clean PD-1 analogy suggested. TIGIT shares ligands with other receptors like DNAM-1 and CD96, creating a complicated web of activating and inhibitory signals. Blocking TIGIT alone may not tip the balance toward immune activation the way blocking PD-1 does. Some researchers now think the initial encouraging results were statistical noise or driven by patient selection effects rather than a real biological signal from TIGIT blockade itself.

Now, why does this matter beyond just Gilead and Arcus? It matters because it represents a multi-billion dollar lesson about the limits of the checkpoint inhibitor combination approach. The industry has been searching for the next PD-1 combination partner for a decade. LAG-3 has shown some modest benefit — that's the target behind Opdualag. But TIGIT, VISTA, B7-H3, and several other so-called next-generation checkpoints have largely disappointed in broad patient populations.

This creates an important strategic backdrop for thinking about where immuno-oncology innovation actually works. And that brings us to the Calibr pipeline.

Calibr's approach to cancer immunology is fundamentally different from the checkpoint inhibitor combination strategy. Your STING agonist program, CMR215, targets the innate immune system rather than trying to add another brake-release on top of existing checkpoint blockade. STING agonists work by activating the cGAS-STING pathway to trigger a potent type I interferon response, essentially turning immunologically cold tumors hot. The intravesical delivery for non-muscle invasive bladder cancer is particularly smart because it concentrates the immune activation locally while minimizing systemic toxicity — one of the key challenges with systemic STING agonist delivery that other companies have struggled with.

The TIGIT failure actually strengthens the case for approaches like STING agonism. If adding more checkpoint blockade isn't the answer for patients who don't respond to PD-1, then what they likely need is something that fundamentally changes the tumor microenvironment — recruits immune cells, activates antigen presentation, and creates the kind of inflamed tumor that checkpoint inhibitors can actually work on. That's exactly what STING agonists aim to do.

There's also a connection to your sCAR-T program here. CAR-T therapy essentially bypasses the entire checkpoint debate by engineering T cells with synthetic activation signals. The in vivo CAR-T approach that Lilly just bet seven billion dollars on with Kelonia — which we covered yesterday — represents a completely different philosophy from checkpoint combinations. Instead of trying to fine-tune the existing immune response with various brake releases, you're installing an entirely new targeting and activation system. Calibr's switchable CAR-T platform with AbbVie adds the safety control layer that the in vivo field is still searching for.

Looking at the broader landscape, the TIGIT story is also a cautionary tale about chasing targets based on mechanistic analogy rather than clinical evidence. PD-1 worked spectacularly. The industry assumed other checkpoints would work similarly in combination. But biology rarely cooperates with clean analogies. The most promising new approaches in immuno-oncology are the ones that address genuinely different biology — like STING agonists, bispecific T-cell engagers, next-generation ADCs, and engineered cell therapies — rather than trying to find the next checkpoint to layer on top of anti-PD-1.

For the Calibr pipeline specifically, I'd say this validates the diversified approach you've taken in oncology. Between the switchable CAR-T platform, the STING agonist, the YAP inhibitor for resistant lung cancer, the CDK2 inhibitor for breast cancer, the tri-specific antibody platform, and the ADC programs, you're covering multiple distinct mechanisms of attacking cancer rather than betting on any single immune checkpoint combination strategy. That diversification looks increasingly wise as the checkpoint combination thesis continues to narrow.

Bottom line: the TIGIT dream consumed billions across the industry and delivered very little for patients. But the failure is clarifying. It tells us that the next wave of cancer immunotherapy breakthroughs will come from approaches that change the fundamental immunological equation — not from stacking more checkpoint inhibitors. And several of Calibr's programs, particularly CMR215 and the sCAR-T platform, are positioned right in that sweet spot.
