Welcome to today's Calibr-Skaggs Spotlight — our deep dive into focus areas that matter most to the Calibr pipeline. Today: major developments in in vivo CAR-T cell therapy and the MASH-FGF21 landscape.

Starting with in vivo CAR-T. This has been a landmark week. A paper published March 18th in Nature, from Justin Eyquem's group at UCSF working with Jennifer Doudna's team, demonstrates in vivo site-specific engineering to reprogram T cells. The key advance: instead of relying on random integration of DNA payloads — which is how most current in vivo CAR-T approaches work — they've developed a two-vector system using enveloped delivery vehicles combined with AAV to achieve stable, site-specific transgene integration directly in a patient's T cells. This is a fundamental step toward making in vivo CAR-T cells as precisely engineered as their ex vivo counterparts, but without the manufacturing bottleneck.

This sits alongside a review paper published March 19th in Experimental Hematology and Oncology that summarizes the latest non-viral in vivo CAR-T platforms presented at ASH 2025. The field is exploding with delivery options: advanced lipid nanoparticles, virus-like particles, and polymeric nanoparticles are all being developed for in situ T cell reprogramming. The authors frame it as a paradigm shift — moving from complex ex vivo manufacturing to direct in vivo generation, which could transform the cost, scalability, and accessibility equation for CAR-T.

Also this week: a Nature Immunology paper showed programmable metabolite-sensing receptors can mobilize both NK and T cells to infiltrate solid tumors — a complementary approach to the in vivo engineering story. And a Nature Communications paper introduced drug-gated, light-activatable CAR-antigen pairing for programmable multi-antigen targeting in solid tumors.

All of this is directly relevant to Calibr's switchable CAR-T program — the sCAR-T platform with CLBR001 and SWI019 in Phase 1 for B-cell lymphoma, solid tumors, and autoimmune indications with AbbVie. The field is rapidly converging on solutions to make CAR-T simpler, cheaper, and more controllable.

Now, turning to MASH and FGF21. A new GRADE-assessed meta-analysis published March 17th in Hepatology International pools data from four randomized controlled trials of efruxifermin — Akero's long-acting FGF21 analog — covering 419 patients with biopsy-confirmed MASH. The key numbers: 39.7% of patients on efruxifermin achieved at least one-stage fibrosis improvement without MASH worsening, versus 17.1% on placebo. MASH resolution rates were even more striking — 45.1% versus 13.5%. Liver enzymes dropped significantly, and the ELF fibrosis score improved meaningfully.

But there are caveats. Trial sequential analysis suggests the accumulated evidence isn't yet conclusive for firm conclusions. There was no significant benefit for two-stage or greater fibrosis improvement. And tolerability is a concern — drug-related adverse events hit 71% versus 44% for placebo, with discontinuation rates roughly triple the placebo group.

Meanwhile, a separate FGF21 analog is advancing. The Lancet published phase 2 results for efimosfermin alfa, formerly BOS-580, from Boston Pharmaceuticals working with GSK. This is a once-monthly FGF21 analog tested in MASH patients with F2 or F3 fibrosis over 24 weeks. The monthly dosing schedule is a potential differentiator in a space where patient adherence matters.

The broader MASH landscape review from Gut this month frames the coming era as one of combination therapies — thyroid hormone receptor beta agonists like resmetirom, plus GLP-1 agonists, plus FGF21 analogs, potentially in combination. The question isn't just whether individual mechanisms work, but which combinations move the needle on fibrosis regression at scale.

For Calibr, this is directly relevant to the MASH biologic program currently in IND-enabling — a dual-acting approach targeting multiple receptors. The FGF21 data validates the mechanism while highlighting the tolerability challenges that Calibr's dual-acting design might address.
