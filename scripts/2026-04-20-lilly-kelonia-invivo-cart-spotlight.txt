In vivo approaches promise off-the-shelf, injectable CAR-T therapies that could be given like a standard infusion.

Now here's the critical nuance. The in vivo CAR-T field is splitting into distinct technological approaches, and they have very different safety profiles.

First, you have mRNA-LNP approaches — companies like CREATE Medicines, which just presented impressive data at AACR here in San Diego. Their all-RNA RetroT platform can engineer CAR-T cells in vivo without viral vectors. The advantage is transient expression — the CAR fades over time, which is a built-in safety valve. The downside is you need repeat dosing, and the durability question remains open.

Second, there are viral vector approaches — like AstraZeneca's acquired EsoBiotec lentiviral platform. These offer stable, permanent integration, but the AstraZeneca-published Nature Medicine data from ESO-T01 showed alarming toxicities — serious immune reactions in all five patients, including one death. Permanent integration means permanent risk.

Third, circular RNA approaches — like Lilly's Orna platform and the recent Circio-Acuitas partnership. Circular RNA is more stable than linear mRNA, offering longer expression windows, but it's still fundamentally transient.

And fourth — and this is where Calibr sits — you have the switchable architecture. Calibr's sCAR-T platform with CLBR001 and the SWI019 switch is philosophically different from all of these. Instead of asking "how do we make the CAR go away if there's a problem," Calibr asks "how do we control the CAR in real time?" The switch molecule acts as a rheostat — you can titrate the dose of SWI019 to dial CAR-T activity up or down. Stop dosing the switch and the CAR-T cells are still there but inactive. Resume the switch and they reactivate.

This is profoundly important in the context of what Lilly just did. Here's why.

The biggest unsolved problem in the in vivo field is safety control. When you inject mRNA or a viral vector directly into a patient to create CAR-T cells in vivo, you don't fully control how many cells get transduced, where in the body they activate, or how strong the immune response will be. The AstraZeneca ESO-T01 data showed exactly this problem — a novel two-phase immune activation pattern that wasn't predicted from preclinical models.

Calibr's switch mechanism could be the answer the in vivo field is searching for. Imagine combining an in vivo delivery platform — whether mRNA-LNP, circular RNA, or something else — with a switchable CAR architecture. You'd get the manufacturing and access advantages of in vivo delivery plus the real-time controllability of the switch. That's potentially the best of both worlds.
