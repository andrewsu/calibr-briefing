Calibr's switchable CAR-T platform, the sCAR-T system using CLBR001 plus SWI019, is currently in Phase 1 with AbbVie for both B-cell lymphoma and autoimmune diseases. The switchable design is what sets it apart from conventional CAR-T. In a standard CAR-T, once those engineered cells are infused, you've essentially handed over the keys — they activate whenever they encounter their target, and if something goes wrong, like cytokine release syndrome or prolonged B-cell aplasia, your main tool is to wait it out or try to kill the CAR-T cells.

The sCAR-T system works differently. CLBR001 cells are the CAR-T cells, but they don't directly bind CD19. Instead, they bind SWI019, a separate adapter molecule that acts as the bridge to CD19 on B cells. This means you control the therapy by controlling the dose and timing of the switch molecule. Want to dial up activity? Give more switch. Want to pause the therapy if side effects emerge? Stop the switch. This dose-titratable, controllable approach could be transformative for autoimmune applications specifically.

Why? Because autoimmune patients aren't cancer patients. In oncology, the risk-benefit calculation often favors aggressive therapy because the disease itself is immediately life-threatening. In autoimmune diseases, patients may live for decades managing their conditions. The safety bar is higher. Physicians and patients want effective B-cell depletion without the uncertainty of a permanently activated, uncontrollable cell therapy running around in the bloodstream.

Today's multi-disease remission data actually strengthens the case for Calibr's approach.
