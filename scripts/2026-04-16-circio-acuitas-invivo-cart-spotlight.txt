Today we're going deep on a story that landed right at the intersection of Calibr's core focus areas: Circio Biotechnologies and Acuitas Therapeutics have announced a collaboration to develop in vivo CAR-T cell therapy using circular RNA delivered via lipid nanoparticles.

Let me unpack why this matters.

So the partnership pairs Circio's circular RNA technology with Acuitas' lipid nanoparticle delivery platform — the same LNP technology that powered the Pfizer-BioNTech COVID vaccine. The concept is elegant: instead of pulling a patient's T cells out of their body, engineering them in a lab over days or weeks, and infusing them back — the traditional ex vivo CAR-T approach — you inject an LNP carrying circular RNA that encodes the CAR construct directly into the patient. Their T cells get reprogrammed in vivo, in their own body, with no apheresis, no cleanroom manufacturing, no vein-to-vein timeline measured in weeks.

Circular RNA is the key innovation here. Unlike linear mRNA, circular RNA lacks free ends, which means it resists degradation by exonucleases. That translates to longer-lasting protein expression — potentially days rather than hours. For a CAR-T application, that means the CAR could be expressed on T cells long enough to mount a therapeutic response, while still being transient. The CAR expression eventually fades, which is actually a feature, not a bug, because it provides an inherent safety mechanism — no permanent genetic modification, no risk of insertional mutagenesis, and a natural off switch as the circular RNA degrades.

Now, let's talk about why this is directly relevant to Calibr.

Your sCAR-T platform — CLBR001 plus SWI019, in Phase 1 with AbbVie — takes a different but philosophically related approach to the same fundamental problem: how do you make CAR-T therapy safer and more controllable? Calibr's answer is the switchable CAR. The CAR-T cells are engineered ex vivo, but they require a separate small molecule switch, SWI019, to activate. No switch, no killing. This gives you dose-titratable control — you can dial the CAR-T activity up or down by adjusting the switch dose.

The Circio-Acuitas approach solves controllability differently: through transience. The CAR expression is temporary by design because circular RNA eventually degrades. So you get a pulse of CAR-T activity, and if you need more, you re-dose the LNP.

Both approaches are responses to the same clinical reality: conventional CAR-T therapies are powerful but carry serious risks. Cytokine release syndrome, neurotoxicity, prolonged B-cell aplasia — these are significant safety concerns, especially as the field pushes into autoimmune diseases where patients aren't terminally ill.

And that's the critical context here. The autoimmune CAR-T wave is accelerating fast. Kyverna's KYV-101, Cabaletta's CABA-201, the Gilead-Ouro gamgertamig program — everyone is racing to bring CD19-targeted CAR-T into lupus, myasthenia gravis, inflammatory myopathies. But autoimmune patients have a much lower risk tolerance than late-stage cancer patients. A therapy that can kill you is acceptable if the alternative is dying from lymphoma. It's a much harder sell for lupus.

This is where controllable and transient approaches shine. Calibr's switch gives you real-time pharmacological control. The Circio circular RNA approach gives you built-in transience. Both are fundamentally safer architectures for the autoimmune setting.

Now, the competitive landscape. In vivo CAR-T is getting crowded. Capstan Therapeutics, which Roche acquired, is working on LNP-delivered mRNA for in vivo CAR-T. Interius BioTherapeutics has similar programs. Umoja Biopharma was working on lentiviral in vivo delivery before pivoting. And now Circio-Acuitas enters with the circular RNA angle.

But here's what I'd flag for Calibr: the in vivo and switchable approaches aren't necessarily competitors — they could be complementary. Imagine a future where you deliver a switchable CAR construct via circular RNA in lipid nanoparticles. You'd get in vivo engineering — no manufacturing — plus dose-titratable control via the switch. That's the holy grail: an off-the-shelf, injectable, controllable CAR-T therapy. Calibr's switch technology could potentially be layered onto any in vivo CAR-T delivery platform.

There are big open questions for the Circio approach, of course. T-cell-specific targeting of the LNPs is a major challenge — you don't want to transfect liver cells or other off-target tissues. The duration of circular RNA expression needs to be tuned: long enough for efficacy but not so long that it causes chronic toxicity. And we're still early — this is preclinical work, and the translational gap between LNP delivery in mice and humans is real.

But the strategic signal is clear: the field is moving toward making CAR-T therapy look more like a drug and less like a procedure. Whether that's through switchable CARs, in vivo delivery, allogeneic off-the-shelf products, or some combination — the winners will be the platforms that crack the safety and accessibility code.

Calibr's sCAR-T platform with AbbVie is well-positioned in this landscape. The switch mechanism is modality-agnostic — it works regardless of how the CAR-T cells are made. And the Phase 1 data in both oncology and autoimmune indications will be critical proof points as the field evolves.

Bottom line: keep a close eye on Circio-Acuitas. Their circular RNA approach validates the thesis that controllable, transient CAR-T is the future. And that's a thesis Calibr has been building toward from day one.
