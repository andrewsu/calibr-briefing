I want to go deep on in vivo CAR-T today, because this week has seen a remarkable convergence of news that paints a picture of a field at an inflection point — enormous potential, enormous investment, and some very sobering safety data.

Let's start with the big picture. Pharmaceutical Technology published a comprehensive feature today titled "The Innovators Working to Make In Vivo Cell Therapy a Reality," and it catalogs something genuinely unprecedented in cell therapy: multiple billion-dollar big pharma acquisitions of in vivo CAR-T companies, most of them made with very little clinical data. AstraZeneca acquired EsoBiotec for a billion dollars in March 2025. Eli Lilly paid 2.4 billion for Orna Therapeutics in February 2026. AbbVie and Bristol Myers Squibb both made billion-dollar acquisitions in the second half of 2025. The strategic logic is clear: if you can reprogram T cells directly inside the patient using a viral vector or lipid nanoparticle, you eliminate the entire ex vivo manufacturing bottleneck — the weeks of cell harvesting, engineering, and expansion that make current CAR-T therapies cost three to five hundred thousand dollars per patient.

But here's where it gets complicated. The first real clinical data from this space has arrived, and it's a mixed bag. A Nature Medicine paper published in late March reported results from AstraZeneca's acquired ESO-T01 program — the EsoBiotec lentiviral in vivo CAR-T targeting BCMA in multiple myeloma. Five patients were treated. Four had objective responses. Three achieved stringent complete remission. The efficacy signal is genuinely impressive for a first-in-human study.

The safety profile, however, is concerning. Every single patient — all five — experienced grade 3 or higher serious adverse events. Four of five developed cytokine release syndrome, three of them severe. One patient had a hyperacute reaction within hours, with cardiovascular instability so severe it forced a mid-trial protocol change to include preemptive steroids. And most sobering: one patient died. While investigators attributed the death to disease progression — spinal cord compression from an expanding tumor — the timing coincided with peak CAR-T expansion and immune activation. The study was ultimately stopped, with no further patients enrolled.

What's particularly striking is the unique toxicity pattern. Unlike conventional ex vivo CAR-T, which triggers immune responses after engineered cells expand over days, ESO-T01 caused an immediate inflammatory surge within 24 hours — before CAR-T cells were even detectable. The researchers believe this is driven by the viral vector itself, introducing a layer of innate immune toxicity that doesn't exist with traditional approaches. Then a second wave of immune activation hit days later. This two-phase response is something entirely new, and current CAR-T toxicity management frameworks may not apply cleanly.

Now, this doesn't mean in vivo CAR-T is dead. Far from it. The field is actively innovating around these challenges. This week, Circio Biotechnologies and Acuitas Therapeutics announced a technology evaluation agreement combining Circio's circular RNA platform with Acuitas' lipid nanoparticle delivery technology for in vivo CAR-T. The key insight here is that LNP-delivered circular RNA represents a fundamentally different approach than the lentiviral vector used in ESO-T01. LNPs may avoid the innate immune activation that caused ESO-T01's hyperacute reactions. And circular RNA is more stable than linear mRNA — resistant to exonuclease degradation — potentially enabling longer-lasting CAR expression without permanent genomic integration.

Meanwhile, Endpoints News reported this week that two RNA-based startups have entered the clinic with in vivo CAR-T approaches specifically targeting autoimmune diseases. This is significant because the autoimmune application may actually be a better fit for transient in vivo CAR-T than oncology — you may only need temporary B-cell depletion to reset the immune system, rather than permanent tumor surveillance.

So where does Calibr's sCAR-T platform fit in all of this? I think the ESO-T01 safety data actually reinforces the value of Calibr's switchable approach. Remember, CLBR001 plus SWI019 is a fundamentally different architecture. Instead of trying to create fully functional CAR-T cells inside the patient in a single shot, the switchable system separates the targeting function from the killing function. The CAR-T cells are engineered ex vivo — maintaining manufacturing control — but their activity is modulated by the switch molecule, SWI019. If a patient develops severe CRS or neurotoxicity, you stop administering the switch and the CAR-T cells go dormant. You can't do that with in vivo approaches — once that lentivirus is injected, there's no off switch.

That controllability becomes even more important as we learn from the ESO-T01 data that in vivo approaches can trigger unpredictable two-phase immune responses. The field is essentially discovering that creating CAR-T cells inside the body introduces immune variables that decades of ex vivo CAR-T experience never encountered.

Now, the competitive landscape is evolving fast. The in vivo and ex vivo approaches may ultimately converge. You could imagine a future where in vivo delivery is combined with switchable control mechanisms — getting the manufacturing simplicity of in vivo CAR-T with the safety controllability of Calibr's switch. That's a space worth watching.

For Calibr's sCAR-T program specifically, the path forward with AbbVie in Phase 1 for B-cell lymphoma, solid tumors, and autoimmune disease is well-positioned. The autoimmune indication in particular is timely — it's exactly where the in vivo players are pivoting, but Calibr has the switch-based safety advantage that the in vivo field is now learning it desperately needs.
