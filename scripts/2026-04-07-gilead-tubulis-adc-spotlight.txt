Let me set the stage. Gilead Sciences announced this morning that it's acquiring Tubulis, a Munich-based biotech, for 3.15 billion dollars upfront, with additional milestone payments that could push the total deal value to around 5 billion. This is Gilead's latest in what has been an aggressive dealmaking streak, and it sends a clear message about where the oncology field is heading.

So what is Gilead buying? Tubulis has developed what many analysts are calling a potentially best-in-class antibody-drug conjugate, or ADC, along with a next-generation linker-payload platform. For those unfamiliar, ADCs are engineered molecules that combine an antibody — which targets a specific protein on cancer cells — with a cytotoxic payload, connected by a chemical linker. The idea is targeted drug delivery: get the chemo to the tumor, spare the rest of the body. The linker technology is critical because premature payload release causes off-target toxicity, which has been the Achilles heel of earlier ADC generations.

Tubulis's platform specifically addresses this linker stability problem. Their proprietary technology creates more stable conjugates, which in preclinical and early clinical work has shown improved therapeutic windows — meaning better efficacy with fewer side effects. That's the holy grail for ADCs.

Now, why does this matter so much to the broader landscape? The ADC space has exploded over the past few years. Daiichi Sankyo and AstraZeneca's Enhertu, an HER2-targeting ADC, essentially rewrote the rules and showed that next-gen ADCs could be transformative in solid tumors. Since then, we've seen a feeding frenzy. Pfizer acquired Seagen for 43 billion dollars back in 2023. AbbVie bought ImmunoGen. Johnson & Johnson grabbed the rights to various ADC assets. The message is clear: every major pharma company wants a seat at the ADC table.

Gilead already had some ADC exposure through its Kite Pharma cell therapy franchise and its existing oncology portfolio, but this Tubulis deal represents a significant escalation. The 5-billion-dollar price tag for a company that's still in relatively early clinical stages tells you how much Gilead values the platform potential, not just the lead candidate.

Now here's where this connects directly to Calibr. Your pipeline includes ADC programs targeting ovarian cancer, non-small cell lung cancer, and small cell lung cancer, currently in the Discovery phase. The Gilead-Tubulis deal validates several things that matter for Calibr's ADC strategy.

Second, the tumor types matter. Tubulis's pipeline, from what's publicly known, is focused on solid tumors — which overlaps with Calibr's ADC targets in lung and ovarian cancer. Third, and this is a broader strategic point: the ADC field is increasingly convergent with other modalities. Calibr's tri-specific antibody platform for solid tumors in breast and lung cancer could potentially be combined with or inform ADC design. The trend in the field is toward multi-functional molecules — bispecific ADCs, ADCs with immune-engaging payloads, and combinations with checkpoint inhibitors or cell therapies. Calibr sits at an interesting intersection here, with programs spanning CAR-T, ADCs, and multi-specific antibodies.
